# Cooperative Finance Framework: From Signup to Property

## The Big Picture

52 families pool $20/month each. Two founding families add $100K+ lump sums.
The cooperative uses a **hybrid insurance model**:

- **Regular members** ($20/mo): carry term life insurance ($100K coverage).
  Death benefits serve as group collateral for borrowing.
- **Founders/whales** ($20/mo + $100K+ lump sum): carry permanent life insurance
  ($250K coverage) through a Life Insurance LLC. Cash value builds over time
  and serves as secondary collateral.

The bundled death benefits + cash value make the group creditworthy enough
to borrow for property, at rates none of them could get alone.

### Entity Structure

```
Whole Life Style Fraternal Benefit Society [501(c)(8)]
+-- Life Insurance LLC (Partnership taxed)
|   +-- Owns permanent life policies on founders/whales
|   +-- Cash value = loanable asset + secondary collateral
|   +-- Buy-sell agreement governs founder exits
+-- Property Holding LLC
|   +-- Owns the real estate
|   +-- Community Land Trust holds the land (optional)
|   +-- Members pay carrying charges or rent
+-- Cooperative Pool (operating account)
    +-- Member contributions ($20/mo)
    +-- Term life premiums paid from here
    +-- Down payment reserves
    +-- Operating expenses
```

### Member Tiers

| Tier | Monthly Cost | Insurance | What They Get |
|------|-------------|-----------|--------------|
| 1: Base | $20/mo | Term life ($100K) | Credit building, voting, pool equity, group borrowing |
| 2: Housing | $20/mo + carrying charges | Term life ($100K) | Tier 1 + unit in property |
| 3: Founder | $20/mo + lump sum ($100K+) | Permanent life ($250K) | Tier 1 + cash value growth + board seat |

This document covers how the whole thing works end-to-end.

## Phase 0: The Bootstrap — Starting with 1-2 Families

You don't need 52 members on day one. You need 1-2 families with conviction and capital.

### What 1 Family with $100K Can Do

**Month 1 (just you):**
- Put $100K into the cooperative pool
- Get a $100K term life policy ($13/mo at age 35)
- Collateral-assign the policy to the cooperative
- Borrowing power: $130K (pool leverage + death benefit)
- You can't do much yet. But the clock starts.

**Month 3 (recruit 5 friends/family):**
- Pool: ~$101K | Death benefit: $600K | Borrowing power: $381K
- Now you have a story: "I put in $100K, 5 others joined, we have $600K
  in death benefit backing. We're building something."

**Month 6 (10 more join via word of mouth):**
- Pool: ~$103K | Death benefit: $1.6M | Borrowing power: $882K
- 16 members. The leverage ratio is 8.8x. A lender starts to listen.

**Month 12 (15 more, track record established):**
- Pool: ~$106K | Death benefit: $3.1M | Borrowing power: $1.6M
- You have 31 members, 12 months of on-time contributions on record,
  and credit bureau reporting has boosted everyone's scores.

**Month 18 (20 more, approaching scale):**
- Pool: ~$110K | Death benefit: $5.1M | Borrowing power: $2.6M
- 51 members. Ready to approach lenders for property.

### What 2 Families with $100K Each Can Do

Everything above, but faster and more credible:
- Month 1: $200K pool, $200K death benefit, $261K borrowing power
- Month 18: $218K pool, $5.2M death benefit, $2.77M borrowing power
- Having two founders also provides redundancy — if one founder has
  a life event, the other keeps the cooperative running.

### Why the Founders' $100K Is Not At Risk

The founder's equity tracks their contribution. At month 18 with 2 founders:
- Each founder has ~$105K in equity (their $100K + returns)
- Their equity is a pro-rata claim on the pool
- The operating agreement protects their stake
- They can exit and withdraw their vested equity at any time
- The death benefit provides additional protection

### The Recruitment Flywheel

Each new member who joins:
1. Adds $100K to the death benefit (for just $13/mo in premiums)
2. Adds $7/mo to the pool (their contribution minus premium)
3. Increases borrowing power by ~$50K (death benefit leverage)
4. Gets credit reporting benefits immediately (incentive to join)
5. Gets life insurance their family needs anyway

**The pitch to member #6:** "5 families already in. $500K in death benefit.
Your $20/mo gets you life insurance AND credit building AND access to
group borrowing power. What are you paying for life insurance now? Nothing?
Your family has zero protection. Join and fix that."

**The pitch at month 12:** "31 families, $3.1M backing, 12-month track record.
We're about to buy our first property. Get in now while membership is open."

### Minimum Viable Cooperative

You can legally form with just 1 member, but practically:
- **7 members** is the minimum for a federal credit union charter
- **10-15 members** is where the death benefit starts to matter to lenders
  ($1-1.5M in coverage)
- **30+ members** is where you can credibly approach lenders for property
  ($3M+ in coverage, meaningful contribution history)

## Phase 1: Formation (Months 0-3)

### Legal Entity

Start as a **mutual benefit corporation** (simplest to form, every state has a
statute for this). File articles of incorporation with the state. Later, apply
for **501(c)(8) fraternal benefit society** status with the IRS (Form 1024)
once the insurance benefit system is operational.

**Why 501(c)(8)?**
- Tax-exempt on investment income
- Legally authorized to provide life insurance benefits to members
- Member-owned, democratic governance
- Profits returned to members/community
- Precedent: Knights of Columbus, Modern Woodmen, Woodmen of the World

**Requirements for 501(c)(8):**
- Lodge system (parent org + local chapters with regular meetings)
- Common bond among members (community, neighborhood, profession, faith)
- Written membership standards, tiers, admission process
- Established system for providing life, sick, accident, or other benefits
- File Form 1024 with IRS (no deadline, but do it early)
- Annual Form 990 disclosure after approval

### Governing Documents

**Operating Agreement** must define:
- Member classes (regular @ $20/mo, whale with lump sums)
- Contribution schedules and obligations
- Equity formula (pro-rata to contributions, with time-weighting optional)
- Voting rights (one member, one vote — not dollar-weighted)
- How the pool can be used (lending, property acquisition, etc.)
- Reserve requirements (minimum % kept liquid)
- Decision thresholds (simple majority for operations, supermajority for property)

**Buy-Sell Agreement** must define:
- What happens when a member wants to leave
- Vesting schedule (e.g., 20% per year over 5 years)
- Buyout formula (equity at time of exit, minus unvested portion)
- What happens when a member dies (death benefit covers obligations first)
- What happens when a member defaults on contributions
- Grace periods and cure provisions
- Dispute resolution (mediation → arbitration → court)

### Member Onboarding

Each member must:
1. Sign the operating agreement
2. Complete financial literacy training (MAF model proves this works)
3. Obtain a term life insurance policy (cooperative helps facilitate group rates)
4. Execute a collateral assignment form (filed with the insurance company)
5. Set up automatic monthly contribution (ACH from bank account)
6. Designate beneficiaries for their policy (family gets remainder after any coop claim)

## Phase 2: Building the Pool (Months 1-24)

### Monthly Flow — What $20/mo Gets You

```
Member pays $20/month
  → $9.10 goes to term life premium (group rate, 30% off individual)
  → $10.90 goes to cooperative savings pool

  What you get immediately:
  - $100,000 life insurance for your family (worth $13/mo individually)
  - Credit bureau reporting (builds your credit score)
  - Membership in the cooperative (voting rights, future housing access)

Founder pays $20/month + $100K lump sum (month 1)
  → $9.10 goes to term life premium
  → $100,010.90 goes to pool (month 1), $10.90/month thereafter
```

### The Group Insurance Discount

Buying term life as a group (association/fraternal rate) saves ~30% vs individual:
- Individual rate at age 35: ~$13/mo for $100K coverage
- Group rate at age 35: ~$9.10/mo for $100K coverage
- Savings: $3.90/mo per member ($46.80/year)
- For 52 members: $2,434/year in collective savings

This alone is a concrete selling point: "Join the cooperative and get cheaper
life insurance than you'd get on your own."

### What the Pool Actually Does

Let's be honest about this:
- The pool sits in a **high-yield savings account** earning ~4%/year
- It does NOT generate investment returns or revenue
- It's just cash accumulating — contributions + modest interest
- The pool's purpose is to serve as down payment / reserves for property
- The real financial power comes from the death benefit, not the pool

### The Real Value Stack for a $20/mo Member

| Benefit | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Life insurance (group rate) | $100K coverage for $9.10/mo (saves $3.90 vs buying alone) |
| Credit building | ~168 point score increase (MAF average) |
| Cooperative membership | Vote on how the group's money is used |
| Future housing access | Option to pay carrying charges for a unit |
| Borrowing power (shared) | Share of $2.8M+ group borrowing capacity |

### Credit Building (MAF Model)

If structured like Mission Asset Fund:
- Report member contributions to all 3 credit bureaus
- Each month of on-time $20 payment = positive credit mark
- Average MAF participant gains 168 credit score points
- This makes individual members more creditworthy too

### Death Benefit Accumulation

By month 12:
- 52 members × $100K = **$5.2 million** in death benefit coverage
- All policies collaterally assigned to the cooperative
- This is the group's primary creditworthiness signal to lenders

## Phase 3: First Borrowing (Months 12-36)

### The Pitch to Lenders

The cooperative approaches lenders (community banks, CDFIs, credit unions) with:

1. **$5.2M in collateral-assigned death benefits** — if any borrowing member
   dies, the outstanding loan balance is paid from their death benefit
2. **$200K+ in pooled savings** — demonstrated 12+ month track record
3. **52 members with improving credit scores** — verified via bureau reports
4. **Formal legal entity** with enforceable operating agreement, buy-sell, bylaws
5. **Diversified risk** — probability of enough members dying to threaten the
   loan is actuarially negligible

### Interest Rate Advantage

Secured loans typically run 2-6 percentage points lower than unsecured:
- Unsecured personal loan: 10-20% APR
- Secured with life insurance collateral: 5-8% APR
- With $5.2M death benefit backing a $500K loan: potentially even lower

The key insight: the death benefit coverage is **multiples** of the loan amount.
A lender making a $500K loan backed by $5.2M in death benefits has a
coverage ratio of ~10:1. That's extraordinarily safe.

### What to Borrow For

**Property acquisition** (the primary goal):
- Multi-family housing for members
- Commercial space for member businesses
- Community facilities
- Land for future development

**Loan programs to target:**
- USDA Community Facilities Direct Loan & Grant Program (rural areas)
- SBA 504 loans (commercial real estate, up to $5.5M)
- CDFI-originated loans (if cooperative gets CDFI certification)
- Community bank portfolio loans (relationship-based, more flexible underwriting)
- State housing finance agency programs

## Phase 4: Property Ownership — The Housing Opt-In

### Two Tiers of Membership

Not every member wants or needs housing. The cooperative has two tiers:

**Tier 1: Base Membership ($20/mo)**
- Group term life insurance at discount rate
- Credit bureau reporting
- Voting rights in the cooperative
- Share of pool equity (pro-rata)
- Access to group borrowing power
- This is the default. Everyone starts here.

**Tier 2: Housing Member ($20/mo + carrying charges)**
- Everything in Tier 1, PLUS
- A unit in the cooperative's property
- Monthly carrying charges cover their share of:
  - Mortgage payment (their portion of the group loan)
  - Property taxes
  - Insurance (building, not life)
  - Maintenance/reserves
  - Utilities (shared or individual)

### How Carrying Charges Work

The cooperative buys a property using its borrowing power (death benefit
collateral). Not all 52 members live there — maybe 10-15 units.

```
Example: Cooperative buys a $500K property (10-unit building)
  Down payment: $100K from pool (20%)
  Mortgage: $400K at 5% for 30 years = $2,147/mo
  Property taxes: ~$500/mo
  Insurance: ~$200/mo
  Maintenance reserve: ~$300/mo
  Total building cost: ~$3,147/mo

  Per unit (10 units): ~$315/mo carrying charge

Compare to market rent for same unit: $800-1,200/mo
Savings: $485-885/mo vs renting
```

### Who Gets a Unit?

Members who want housing opt in by:
1. Declaring interest to the board
2. Paying a **share purchase** (their portion of the down payment)
   - e.g., $100K down / 10 units = $10K per unit share
   - Can be paid upfront or financed from their pool equity
3. Committing to monthly carrying charges
4. Priority can be based on: seniority in the cooperative, contribution
   history, lottery among qualified members, or board decision

### The Math for a Housing Member

```
Monthly costs:
  Base membership:     $  20.00  (life insurance + pool contribution)
  Carrying charges:    $ 315.00  (their share of building costs)
  Total:               $ 335.00

What they'd pay renting: $800-1,200/mo
Net savings:             $465-865/mo

What they're building:
  - Life insurance ($100K coverage for family)
  - Credit score improvement
  - Housing equity (their share purchase appreciates with building)
  - Below-market housing costs
  - Community ownership (they vote on building decisions)
```

### Property Ownership Structure

**Community Land Trust (CLT) + Limited Equity Cooperative (LEC):**

- CLT (nonprofit) owns the land permanently → prevents speculation
- Cooperative owns the building → members own shares
- Ground lease (99-year renewable) connects the two
- Share price is capped (limited equity) → keeps it affordable
- If a housing member leaves, they sell their share back at the capped price
- New member buys in at the same affordable price

**Why limited equity?** If shares were unlimited, early members could sell
at market rate and price out future members. The cap ensures the housing
stays affordable across generations. Members still build equity — just
not speculative windfall equity.

### Non-Housing Members Still Benefit

Members who don't live in the property still benefit:
- Their pool contributions helped fund the down payment
- The property generates value for the cooperative as a whole
- If the cooperative acquires more properties, they can opt in later
- Their $20/mo still gets them insurance + credit + membership

## Phase 5: Risk Management

### What Happens When a Member Dies

1. Death benefit ($100K) pays out to the cooperative (as assignee)
2. Cooperative takes only what's owed (pro-rata share of outstanding loans)
3. Remaining death benefit goes to member's family
4. Member's equity in the cooperative is bought out per buy-sell agreement
5. New member can be admitted to fill the slot

**Example:**
- Member dies with $8K in cooperative equity
- Cooperative has $500K loan outstanding, member's share = ~$9,600
- Death benefit pays $9,600 to cooperative to cover loan share
- Remaining $90,400 goes to member's family
- Family also receives the $8K equity buyout

### What Happens When a Member Defaults

**Cure period:** 60-90 days of missed contributions before action.

**Escalation:**
1. Friendly reminder (automated, month 1)
2. Peer outreach — another member checks in (month 2)
3. Formal notice of default with cure period (month 3)
4. If uncured: member's equity is frozen, voting rights suspended
5. Board vote on forced buyout (supermajority required)
6. Equity returned minus unvested portion and any amounts owed
7. Collateral assignment on their policy is released
8. New member can be admitted

**Why this works better than informal ROSCAs:**
- Formal legal entity = enforceable contracts
- Contributions via ACH = consistent, automated
- Vesting schedule = incentive to stay
- Credit reporting = defaulting hurts their credit score
- Peer accountability + formal structure = best of both worlds

### What Happens When a Member Wants to Leave Voluntarily

1. Submit written notice (30-60 days)
2. Board processes buyout per formula in operating agreement
3. Vested equity returned (minus any outstanding obligations)
4. Collateral assignment on their policy released
5. Slot opened for new member admission
6. No penalty beyond unvested equity

### Insurance Company Risk

What if the insurance company fails?
- State guaranty associations cover term life up to $300K in most states
- Diversify across 2-3 carriers to spread risk
- Only A-rated or better carriers

## The Math That Makes It Work

### For a $20/month member over 10 years:

| What They Pay | Amount |
|---------------|--------|
| Total contributions | $2,400 |
| Of which: premiums | $1,560 (65%) |
| Of which: to pool | $840 (35%) |

| What They Get | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Life insurance coverage | $100,000 (for their family) |
| Pool equity (with returns) | ~$2,900 |
| Credit score improvement | ~168 points (MAF average) |
| Access to group borrowing | Share of $2.9M borrowing power |
| Housing (if property acquired) | Below-market carrying charges |

### For the collective over 10 years:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total death benefit backing | $5,200,000 |
| Pool value | ~$441,000 |
| Borrowing power | ~$2,950,000 |
| Total contributed | $364,800 |
| Properties acquirable | $500K-$2M+ depending on leverage |

### The leverage ratio:

- $364,800 in total contributions → $2,950,000 in borrowing power
- That's an **8:1 leverage ratio**
- Achieved through the death benefit, not through risky financial engineering
- The collateral is real, actuarially sound, and legally enforceable

## Revenue Model (Post-Property)

The property generates rental income from non-member tenants:

```
10-unit building:
  6 units: housing members (pay carrying charges ~$335/mo)
  4 units: market-rate or below-market rental (~$900/mo)

Revenue:
  6 x $335 = $2,008/mo (covers their share of mortgage)
  4 x $900 = $3,600/mo (rental income to cooperative)
  Total:     $5,608/mo

Expenses:
  Mortgage:     $2,147/mo
  Taxes:        $500/mo
  Insurance:    $200/mo
  Maintenance:  $300/mo
  Management:   $200/mo
  Total:        $3,347/mo

Net income:     $2,261/mo -> $27,133/year back to cooperative
```

Revenue comes from **being a landlord** with below-market internal rents
subsidized by market-rate external rents. Not from investment returns.

### Lender Metrics

- **Traditional DSCR**: NOI / Annual Debt Service = ~2.05 (target >= 1.25)
- **Adjusted DSCR**: (NOI + Member Contributions) / Debt Service = ~2.54
- **Coverage Ratio**: $5.5M death benefit / $400K loan = 13.75:1

## Micro-Cooperative Path: Starting as a Couple

You don't need 50 members to start. A married couple can bootstrap the cooperative
and grow one group at a time. The death benefit collateral exists from policy day 1.

### The Micro Growth Schedule

| Stage | Month | Members | Description |
|-------|-------|---------|-------------|
| Founders | 1 | 2 | The couple — buy term life, $50/mo each |
| Group 1 | 6 | 7 | Inner Circle — family and close friends |
| Group 2 | 12 | 12 | Word of Mouth — first results visible |
| Group 3 | 24 | 22 | Track Record — one-year proof of concept |
| Group 4 | 36 | 37 | Community Expansion |
| Group 5 | 48 | 52 | Full Scale — property-ready |

### Key Differences at Small Scale

- **No group discount under 10 members**: Individual term life rates apply (~$13/mo vs $9/mo group)
- **Partial discount at 10-24 members**: 15% off individual rates
- **Full group discount at 25+ members**: 30% off (standard group rate)
- **Premiums are higher per person early on**, but death benefit collateral works the same
- **With $100K lump sum**: Founders can opt into permanent life via Life Insurance LLC
- **Without lump sum**: Founders carry term life like regular members, just at higher monthly contribution

### Running the Micro Simulation

```bash
# Basic: couple bootstrapping with $50/mo each, no lump sum
uv run python -m coopsim --growth --micro --instruments hybrid --months 60

# With lump sum: couple with $100K each
uv run python -m coopsim --growth --micro --founder-amount 100000 --instruments hybrid

# Custom founder contribution
uv run python -m coopsim --growth --micro --founder-monthly 100 --instruments term_life

# Full lifecycle: micro growth → property acquisition
uv run python -m coopsim --growth --micro --property 400000 --units 4 --housing-members 2 --rental-units 2
```

## Why This Hasn't Been Done (Much)

1. **Regulatory complexity** — straddling insurance, banking, and real estate law
2. **Trust deficit** — informal ROSCAs work on social trust, scaling requires legal trust
3. **No off-the-shelf tooling** — need custom ledger, simulation, and reporting
4. **Chicken-and-egg** — need members to show lenders, need lending proof to get members
5. **Insurance industry friction** — group term life is usually employer-sponsored

### What's Changed

- **Fintech platforms** (like MAF) have proven digital lending circles work at scale
- **Credit reporting integration** solves the trust/accountability problem
- **Community land trusts** are now well-established legal models
- **CDFI ecosystem** provides capital and technical assistance
- **This simulator** lets you model and demonstrate the math before anyone commits
